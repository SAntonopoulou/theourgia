# Digital Inheritance and Memorial Mode

A magical record is a life's work, and lives end. Memorial mode is how
you decide, ahead of time and on your own terms, what happens to your
vault when you are no longer here to tend it. Setting it up takes a few
minutes, it is entirely optional, and every part of it can be changed
or undone while you are alive. Think of it as a friend helping you
think ahead — nothing here is urgent, and nothing is watching you.

Everything below lives at `/memorial-mode`.

## The check-in rhythm

You choose a cadence — how many days between check-ins — and a warning
window. The status card at the top of the page always tells you where
things stand:

- **This vault is active** — all is well; it shows roughly how long
  until your next suggested check-in.
- **Time to check in soon** — the cadence has lapsed and the warning
  window is counting.
- **Check-in overdue** — the warning window has passed too. If you're
  here, a quick check-in resets everything.

Checking in is one button: **"I'm here."** That is the whole ceremony.
Setting the cadence to zero disables the schedule entirely — you can
keep the executor details and message on file without any clock running.

Entering memorial mode is itself a deliberate act, not something that
happens behind your back: the page carries an explicit
"Enter memorial mode now" action, and the check-in states exist so that
the people you designate know when that decision has become theirs
to consider.

## Your digital executor

Name a person you trust — a friend, a partner, a family member — and
record their email. For now this is a contact field: a place where the
right name is written down. A cryptographic key-share, letting an
executor unlock designated material through a guided process, is planned
for a future update.

## The memorial message

You can write a message that visitors will see if memorial mode ever
activates. Whatever you want them to read. Blank is fine — the vault
is enough.

## What "memorialized" means

If memorial mode activates, the vault becomes a read-only, in-memoriam
surface:

- **Public content stays public.** What you published remains readable,
  exactly as you published it.
- **Private content stays private.** Nothing that was personal becomes
  visible because you died.
- **Sealed content stays sealed, forever.** Sealed material is encrypted
  with keys derived from your passphrase, which existed only in your
  head and on your device. No one — not your executor, not the server
  operator, not the software — can open it. If you sealed an oath, it
  goes with you. This is not a limitation; it is the promise the seal
  made you when you closed it.

Memorial mode is also reversible: if the vault was memorialized in error
— or you simply come back — the operator can reactivate it from the same
page.

## Posthumous publication

A single switch controls whether entries marked for release after death
are auto-published when memorial mode activates. It is off by default;
if it is off, nothing is ever published on your behalf.

## Have the conversation

The settings only work if someone knows they exist. Sometime soon, tell
your executor, in plain words:

- that this vault exists, and where it lives;
- that you have named them, and what the memorial message says;
- what you want to happen — and just as important, what you want left
  alone;
- that the sealed material is sealed by mathematics, not by policy, and
  no one will be able to open it — so they never have to wonder whether
  they should try.

It is a strange conversation to start and a kind one to have had. Ten
minutes over tea covers it, and then it is done, and you can go back to
the work.
